## Domain Knowledge

### Legal Structures

Indian NGOs can be registered under three primary legal frameworks:

#### 1. Trust (Indian Trusts Act, 1882 / State-specific Acts)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Governing law** | Indian Trusts Act, 1882 (for private trusts); various state-specific Public Trusts Acts (e.g., Bombay Public Trusts Act, 1950 for Maharashtra and Gujarat) |
| **Registration** | Registered with the local Sub-Registrar or Charity Commissioner (varies by state) |
| **Governing body** | Board of Trustees |
| **Minimum members** | 2 trustees (varies by state) |
| **Key document** | Trust Deed |
| **Amendment** | Trust Deed is generally difficult to amend; requires court/Charity Commissioner approval in many states |
| **Dissolution** | Difficult; trust property must be applied to similar charitable purposes |
| **Best suited for** | Long-term endowments; family-led philanthropic organizations; organizations focused on specific geographies |

#### 2. Society (Societies Registration Act, 1860)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Governing law** | Societies Registration Act, 1860; various state amendments |
| **Registration** | Registered with the Registrar of Societies (state-level) |
| **Governing body** | Managing Committee / Executive Committee (elected by General Body) |
| **Minimum members** | 7 members (for registration) |
| **Key document** | Memorandum of Association and Rules & Regulations (Bye-laws) |
| **Amendment** | Relatively easier; General Body resolution + Registrar approval |
| **Dissolution** | General Body resolution; assets applied to similar purposes |
| **Best suited for** | Membership-based organizations; professional associations; organizations with democratic governance |

#### 3. Section 8 Company (Companies Act, 2013, Section 8)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Governing law** | Companies Act, 2013 (Section 8); MCA regulations |
| **Registration** | Registered with the Registrar of Companies (MCA) |
| **Governing body** | Board of Directors |
| **Minimum members** | 2 directors and 2 members (private limited); 3 directors and 7 members (limited) |
| **Key document** | Memorandum of Association and Articles of Association |
| **Amendment** | Governed by Companies Act procedures; requires ROC filing |
| **Dissolution** | As per Companies Act winding-up provisions |
| **Best suited for** | Organizations seeking corporate-level governance; those receiving large institutional grants; CSR implementing agencies (often preferred by corporate donors) |

#### Choosing a Legal Structure

| Consideration | Trust | Society | Section 8 Company |
|--------------|-------|---------|-------------------|
| **Ease of registration** | Simple | Moderate | Complex |
| **Governance rigor** | Low (trustee-led) | Moderate (democratic) | High (Companies Act) |
| **Compliance burden** | Low | Moderate | High (annual filings, audit, Board meetings) |
| **Donor preference** | Variable | Common | Increasingly preferred by corporate CSR |
| **FCRA eligibility** | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| **12A/80G eligibility** | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| **Flexibility to amend** | Low | Moderate | Moderate |
| **Transparency** | Low (state-dependent) | Moderate | High (public filings on MCA) |

### Registration Requirements

An operational Indian NGO typically needs the following registrations:

#### 12A Registration (Income Tax Act, 1961)

- **Purpose:** Exempts the organization's income from income tax
- **Issued by:** Income Tax Department (Principal Commissioner/Commissioner)
- **Section:** 12A/12AB of the Income Tax Act
- **Post-2020 amendment:** New registration process under Section 12AB; existing 12A registrations had to be renewed/re-registered
- **Validity:** 5 years (renewable); provisional registration for 3 years for new organizations
- **Requirement:** The organization must apply its income to charitable purposes; not more than 15% can be accumulated (balance must be spent or applied)
- **Key condition:** Must file ITR (Income Tax Return) annually, even if income is exempt

#### 80G Registration (Income Tax Act, 1961)

- **Purpose:** Allows donors to the organization to claim tax deduction on their donations
- **Issued by:** Income Tax Department (Principal Commissioner/Commissioner)
- **Section:** 80G of the Income Tax Act
- **Benefit to donors:** Deduction of 50% of the donation from taxable income (for most organizations); some specified funds get 100% deduction
- **Validity:** 5 years (renewable); provisional for 3 years for new organizations
- **Key condition:** Organization must be registered under 12A; must not have income from business activities exceeding 20% of total receipts

#### FCRA Registration

- See `fcra-compliance.md` for detailed coverage
- **Purpose:** Permits the organization to receive foreign contributions
- **Issued by:** Ministry of Home Affairs (MHA)
- **Validity:** 5 years (renewable)
- **Key requirement:** Must maintain designated FCRA account at SBI New Delhi Main Branch (post-2020 amendment)

#### DARPAN / NGO-DARPAN Registration

- **Purpose:** Unique ID for NGOs in the government's central database; mandatory for receiving government grants
- **Issued by:** NITI Aayog (portal: ngodarpan.gov.in)
- **Validity:** No expiry; but details must be kept updated
- **Key use:** Required for:
  - Receiving grants from Central/State Government ministries
  - PFMS (Public Financial Management System) integration
  - Increasingly used by corporate CSR departments for verification
- **Information captured:** Organization details, Board members, projects, financials, registration documents

#### CSR-1 Registration

- See `csr-compliance.md` for detailed coverage
- **Purpose:** Mandatory for entities implementing CSR projects on behalf of companies
- **Issued by:** MCA (Ministry of Corporate Affairs)
- **Validity:** One-time registration; unique CSR Registration Number
- **Key requirement:** Must be a Section 8 company, registered trust, or registered society

#### Other Registrations

| Registration | Purpose | Authority |
|-------------|---------|-----------|
| **PAN** | Tax identification | Income Tax Department |
| **TAN** | Tax deduction at source (TDS) | Income Tax Department |
| **GST** (if applicable) | Goods and Services Tax (if turnover exceeds threshold or inter-state supply) | GST Council |
| **POSH** | Prevention of Sexual Harassment (Internal Complaints Committee) | Organization's Board |
| **Professional Tax** | Employment tax (state-specific) | State government |
| **Shops and Establishments** | Registration of office premises | Municipal/State authority |
| **EPF/ESI** (if applicable) | Employee Provident Fund / Employee State Insurance | EPFO/ESIC (if 20+ employees for EPF; 10+ for ESI) |

### Governance

#### Board/Governing Body

**Composition best practices:**
- Minimum 5-7 members (even if law permits fewer)
- Mix of expertise: program, finance, legal, sector-specific
- Independent members with no financial interest in the organization
- Gender diversity (aim for at least 30% women)
- No family dominance (maximum 2 related members on a board of 7+)
- Clearly defined roles: Chairperson, Secretary, Treasurer

**Key responsibilities:**
- Strategic direction and oversight
- Approval of annual plan and budget
- Appointment and appraisal of CEO/Executive Director
- Financial oversight and audit
- Policy approval (HR, finance, procurement, safeguarding)
- Compliance oversight (statutory filings, donor compliance)
- Fundraising and stakeholder relationships

**Meeting frequency:**
- Board meetings: Minimum quarterly (best practice); statutory requirement varies
- For Section 8 companies: At least 1 Board meeting every quarter (Companies Act requirement)
- Annual General Meeting (AGM): Once a year (mandatory for societies and Section 8 companies)

#### Key Governance Documents

| Document | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| **Trust Deed / MoA / AoA** | Founding document defining objects, governance structure |
| **Bye-laws / Rules** | Operational rules for governance, meetings, elections |
| **Board Minutes** | Record of all Board decisions; legally important |
| **Conflict of Interest Policy** | Prevents self-dealing; requires disclosure and recusal |
| **Code of Conduct** | Expected behavior for Board members and staff |
| **Financial Policy Manual** | Budgeting, procurement, authorization limits, audit |
| **HR Policy Manual** | Recruitment, compensation, leave, POSH, exit |
| **Safeguarding Policy** | Child protection, vulnerable adults, anti-exploitation |
| **Whistleblower Policy** | Protected channels for reporting misconduct |
| **Data Protection Policy** | Handling of beneficiary and organizational data |

### Financial Management

#### Fund Accounting

NGOs use **fund accounting** rather than commercial accounting. Key concepts:

| Fund Type | Definition | Example |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **Restricted Fund** | Funds designated by the donor for a specific purpose/project | CSR grant for education project in Rajasthan |
| **Unrestricted Fund** | Funds available for general organizational purposes | Individual donations, interest income, core grants |
| **Corpus Fund** | Endowment funds where only interest/income is spent | Corpus donations under Section 11(1)(d) |
| **Capital Fund** | Funds invested in fixed assets | Building, vehicles, equipment |

**Key principle:** Restricted funds can only be used for the specified purpose. Diverting restricted funds is both a donor compliance violation and potentially a legal issue.

#### Financial Statements

Indian NGOs must prepare:
1. **Balance Sheet** — Assets, liabilities, and fund balances
2. **Income and Expenditure Account** — Revenue and expenses for the period (analogous to P&L for commercial entities)
3. **Receipts and Payments Account** — Cash-based summary of all money received and spent
4. **Schedules and Notes** — Supporting details for key items

**Accounting standards:** Indian Accounting Standards (Ind AS) do not have a specific standard for NGOs. Many NGOs follow the ICAI's Guidance Note on Accounting for Not-for-Profit Organizations. Larger organizations increasingly follow Ind AS where applicable.

#### Internal Controls

Essential financial controls for NGOs:

| Control | Purpose |
|---------|---------|
| **Segregation of duties** | Different people authorize, execute, and record transactions |
| **Authorization limits** | Defined approval authorities for different expenditure levels |
| **Bank reconciliation** | Monthly reconciliation of bank statements with books |
| **Physical verification** | Annual physical verification of assets and inventory |
| **Cash management** | Minimize cash transactions; documented cash handling procedures |
| **Voucher system** | Every transaction supported by approved voucher with supporting documents |
| **Budget control** | Expenditure within approved budgets; variance analysis |
| **Audit trail** | Every transaction traceable from voucher to ledger to financial statements |

### HR in NGOs

#### Staff Categories

| Category | Description | Typical Arrangement |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Management/Leadership** | CEO, Directors, Heads of Department | Full-time salaried; Board-appointed |
| **Program Staff** | Program Managers, Coordinators, Specialists | Full-time salaried; organization-appointed |
| **Field Staff** | Field Officers, Community Workers, Mobilizers | Full-time or contractual; often project-funded |
| **Finance and Admin** | Accountants, HR, Admin assistants | Full-time salaried; organizational budget |
| **Technical Consultants** | Subject matter experts, evaluators, trainers | Contractual; project-funded; short-term engagements |
| **Interns and Fellows** | Early-career professionals | Stipend-based; typically 3-12 months |
| **Volunteers** | Unpaid contributors | Community members, corporate volunteers, skilled volunteers |

#### HR Challenges Specific to NGOs
- **Salary parity:** NGO salaries are typically lower than corporate sector; retention is a challenge
- **Project-based employment:** Staff tied to specific grants face uncertainty when grants end
- **Field staff management:** Remote locations, limited supervision, high travel requirements
- **Compliance:** EPF, ESI, TDS, Professional Tax, POSH compliance often under-resourced
- **Capacity building:** Limited budgets for staff training and professional development
- **Safeguarding:** Working with vulnerable populations requires robust safeguarding policies and training

### Procurement and Asset Management

#### Procurement Principles
1. **Transparency:** Open, documented process for all procurements
2. **Value for money:** Best quality at the lowest cost (not just lowest cost)
3. **Competition:** Multiple quotations for significant purchases
4. **Documentation:** Written records of quotations, comparisons, decisions
5. **Authorization:** Procurement within approved budgets and authorization limits
6. **Conflict of interest:** No procurement from Board members, staff, or their relatives without disclosure and approval

#### Procurement Thresholds (Typical)

| Amount | Process |
|--------|---------|
| Up to INR 5,000 | Single quotation; approved by program manager |
| INR 5,001 - 25,000 | Minimum 3 quotations; comparative statement; approved by department head |
| INR 25,001 - 1,00,000 | Minimum 3 written quotations; comparative statement; approved by CEO/Director |
| Above INR 1,00,000 | Formal tender/RFP process; evaluation committee; Board approval for major items |

**Note:** Thresholds vary by organization; these are illustrative. Donor-specific procurement rules may apply for grant-funded purchases.

#### Asset Management
- **Asset register:** All assets above a threshold (e.g., INR 5,000) recorded with: description, date of purchase, cost, funding source, location, custodian, condition
- **Asset tagging:** Physical tag/label on each asset for identification
- **Annual physical verification:** Compare register with physical assets; note discrepancies
- **Depreciation:** As per Income Tax Act or accounting policy
- **Disposal:** Documented process — Board approval for significant assets; sale/donation/scrap; proceeds to appropriate fund
- **Grant-funded assets:** Must be treated as per the Grant Agreement (see `grant-management.md`)

### Program Implementation Models

| Model | Description | When Used |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| **Direct implementation** | Organization directly delivers services through its own staff and infrastructure | Full control needed; own field presence; specialized expertise |
| **Partnership / Sub-granting** | Organization funds and supports partner NGOs who implement on the ground | Scale; geographic spread; leveraging local expertise |
| **Consortium** | Multiple organizations jointly implement, each contributing specific expertise | Complex, multi-thematic programs; large-scale donor programs |
| **Technical assistance** | Organization provides expertise/training to government or other agencies who implement | Government convergence; capacity building; systemic change |
| **Community-led** | Organization facilitates and supports community institutions (SHGs, village committees) who implement | Sustainability; empowerment; rights-based approaches |
| **Hybrid** | Combination of above models | Most large programs use a mix |

### Reporting to Donors and Statutory Bodies

#### Statutory Reporting

| Authority | Requirement | Frequency |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Income Tax Department** | ITR filing (even if exempt); 12A/80G compliance | Annual |
| **Registrar of Societies / Charity Commissioner** | Annual report, list of Managing Committee, financial statements | Annual |
| **MCA (for Section 8)** | AOC-4 (financial statements), MGT-7 (annual return), Board meeting compliance | Annual/Quarterly |
| **MHA (FCRA)** | FC-4 annual return; changes intimation | Annual / As required |
| **NITI Aayog (DARPAN)** | Update organizational details, financials, projects | Annual / As required |
| **EPF/ESI** | Monthly contributions and returns | Monthly |
| **TDS** | Quarterly TDS returns; annual certificates | Quarterly |

#### Donor Reporting
- As per Grant Agreement — see `grant-management.md`
- Typically: quarterly progress reports, SoE, UC, and annual audit reports
- CSR donors additionally require CSR-specific formats and Impact Assessment

### Sustainability Planning

NGO sustainability is the ability to continue delivering impact after specific funding ends.

**Sustainability strategies:**

| Strategy | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Funding diversification** | Multiple donors, funding types (CSR, FCRA, government, retail); reduce dependence on any single source |
| **Corpus building** | Accumulate endowment funds whose interest income supports operations |
| **Fee-for-service** | Charge for training, consulting, or technical services (within charitable purpose limits) |
| **Social enterprise** | Revenue-generating activities aligned with the mission (may need separate legal entity) |
| **Government convergence** | Link program beneficiaries to government schemes; embed program into government systems |
| **Community ownership** | Build community institutions (SHGs, federations, cooperatives) that continue functioning independently |
| **Knowledge products** | Manuals, curricula, toolkits that can be licensed or adapted by others |
| **Volunteer networks** | Trained community volunteers who continue activities without paid staff |

## Key Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **12A** | Income Tax Act registration that exempts NGO income from tax |
| **80G** | Income Tax Act registration that allows donors to claim tax deduction on donations to the NGO |
| **DARPAN** | NITI Aayog's centralized NGO database; registration required for government grants |
| **Schedule VII** | List of eligible CSR activities under Companies Act 2013; relevant for NGOs implementing CSR projects |
| **Implementing Agency** | Organization that executes programs on behalf of a funder |
| **CSR-1** | MCA registration for entities implementing corporate CSR projects |
| **Trust Deed** | Founding document of a registered trust |
| **MoA (Memorandum of Association)** | Founding document of a society or Section 8 company, defining objects and membership |
| **AoA (Articles of Association)** | Governing rules for a Section 8 company (analogous to bye-laws for societies) |
| **General Body** | All members of a society; highest decision-making authority |
| **Managing Committee** | Elected/appointed body that governs a society on behalf of the General Body |
| **Restricted Funds** | Donor-designated funds that must be used for a specific purpose/project |
| **Unrestricted Funds** | Funds available for general organizational use |
| **Corpus Fund** | Endowment where only the income (not principal) is spent |
| **Fund Accounting** | Accounting method used by NGOs to track funds by source and restriction |
| **Charity Commissioner** | State-level authority overseeing public trusts (e.g., in Maharashtra, Gujarat) |
| **Registrar of Societies** | State-level authority registering and overseeing societies |
| **POSH** | Prevention of Sexual Harassment at Workplace Act, 2013 |
| **SHG** | Self-Help Group — community-based group (typically 10-20 women) for savings, credit, and mutual support |
| **CBO** | Community-Based Organization — local organization formed by community members |
| **CSO** | Civil Society Organization — broader term encompassing NGOs, CBOs, and other non-state actors |

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Assuming all NGOs are the same legally.** A Trust, Society, and Section 8 Company have fundamentally different governance structures, compliance requirements, and amendment processes. Advice must be specific to the legal form.

2. **Treating 12A and 80G as interchangeable.** 12A exempts the organization's income from tax. 80G gives the donor a tax deduction. Both are needed, but they serve different purposes and have separate application processes.

3. **Not renewing registrations on time.** 12A, 80G, and FCRA all have expiry dates (5 years). Missing renewal deadlines can result in loss of tax exemption, donor tax deduction, and ability to receive foreign funds. Maintain a compliance calendar.

4. **Weak governance as a red flag.** Donors conducting due diligence look at Board composition, meeting frequency, conflict of interest policies, and governance documents. An NGO with a family-dominated Board, no minutes, and no policies will fail due diligence.

5. **Commingling restricted and unrestricted funds.** Spending restricted grant funds on general expenses is a serious compliance violation. Fund accounting must clearly segregate funds by source and restriction.

6. **Under-investing in compliance infrastructure.** Small and mid-size NGOs often lack dedicated finance and compliance staff, leading to filing delays, audit qualifications, and donor dissatisfaction. Even a part-time compliance consultant is better than no oversight.

7. **Ignoring POSH compliance.** Organizations with 10+ employees must constitute an Internal Complaints Committee. Many NGOs, despite working on gender issues, fail to implement POSH internally.

8. **Procurement without documentation.** Informal procurement ("we always buy from this vendor") is a compliance risk. Even small purchases should follow a documented process with quotations and approvals.

9. **Not planning for sustainability from the start.** Waiting until funding ends to think about sustainability is too late. Sustainability strategies (government convergence, community ownership, funding diversification) must be built into program design from day one.

10. **Treating donor compliance as the goal.** Compliance is necessary but not sufficient. The goal is impact. Organizations that focus solely on ticking compliance boxes while neglecting program quality and beneficiary outcomes miss the point.
