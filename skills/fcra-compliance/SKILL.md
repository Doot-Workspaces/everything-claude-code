# FCRA Compliance (India)

- Distinction between foreign and domestic funding for Indian NGOs
- Foreign donor due diligence on Indian partners

## Domain Knowledge

### Legal Foundation

**Foreign Contribution (Regulation) Act, 2010** (FCRA 2010) replaced the earlier FCRA, 1976. It regulates the acceptance and utilization of foreign contribution by individuals, associations, and companies in India.

**FCRA Amendment Act, 2020** (effective 29 September 2020) introduced significant changes that tightened compliance requirements.

**Foreign Contribution (Regulation) Rules, 2011** (FCRR 2011, amended 2015, 2019, 2020, 2022) provide operational details.

**Administering authority:** Ministry of Home Affairs (MHA), Government of India.

### Who Needs FCRA?

Any **person** (defined broadly to include individuals, Hindu undivided families, associations, and companies) who wishes to accept **foreign contribution** must either:
- Hold a valid FCRA **registration**, or
- Obtain **prior permission** for a specific project/activity

**Foreign contribution** includes:
- Donations, grants, or gifts from a foreign source
- Securities, articles, or currency from a foreign source
- Interest or income earned on foreign contribution

**Foreign source** includes:
- Foreign governments and their agencies
- International agencies (not specified by Central Government)
- Foreign citizens
- Foreign companies
- Companies with more than 50% foreign shareholding
- Subsidiaries of foreign companies
- Foreign trusts, foundations, societies, clubs

### Registration vs Prior Permission

| Aspect | Registration | Prior Permission |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Eligibility** | Associations registered under a statute (society, trust, Section 8 company) AND operational for at least 3 years with reasonable activity in its chosen field | Any person (including newly formed entities) for a specific project |
| **Duration** | 5 years (renewable) | Specific project or up to 5 years, whichever is earlier; now treated as if valid for the project duration |
| **Scope** | General — can receive foreign contribution for any activity in line with its objectives | Specific to the project/donor/amount described in the application |
| **Application form** | FC-3A (online on FCRA portal) | FC-3B (online on FCRA portal) |
| **Processing time** | 90-180 days (can take longer) | 30-90 days (typically faster for well-documented applications) |
| **Renewal** | Required every 5 years; application to be filed 6 months before expiry | Not renewable; fresh application required for new projects |

**Post-2020 amendment:** The concept of renewal has become more stringent. Many organizations have faced delayed renewals, and MHA has periodically extended validity for pending applications.

### Designated FCRA Account

**The most impactful change in the 2020 amendment:**

- Every FCRA-registered or prior-permission entity must open and maintain a designated **FCRA account** at the **State Bank of India (SBI), New Delhi Main Branch (Parliament Street)**
- All foreign contributions must **first be received** in this designated FCRA account
- The entity can then transfer funds to one or more **utilization accounts** at any scheduled bank in India for actual spending
- The utilization accounts must also be registered/intimated on the FCRA portal

**Practical implications:**
- Organizations across India (even in remote areas) must maintain this SBI New Delhi account
- The SBI account serves as a "funnel" that gives MHA real-time visibility into foreign fund inflows
- Interest earned in the FCRA account is also treated as foreign contribution
- The designated account details must be communicated to all foreign donors

### 20% Administrative Expenditure Cap

FCRA mandates that not more than **20% of the foreign contribution received** in a financial year can be spent on administrative expenses.

**What counts as administrative expenditure:**
- Salaries, wages, and travel expenses of personnel engaged in management and administration
- Office rent and utilities for administrative offices
- Expenses on accounting, legal, and professional services for administration
- Stationery, printing, telephone, internet for admin purposes
- Depreciation on assets used for administration
- Vehicle expenses for administrative travel
- Bank charges on the FCRA account

**What does NOT count as administrative:**
- Salaries of program/field staff directly implementing the project
- Travel expenses for program activities (field visits, beneficiary engagement)
- Program materials, supplies, and equipment used in project delivery
- Training and capacity building costs for beneficiaries
- Monitoring and evaluation costs directly attributable to the project

**Key nuance:** The 20% cap is on the total foreign contribution **received** in the year, not on the total expenditure. If an organization receives INR 1 crore in foreign contribution, admin expenses cannot exceed INR 20 lakh — even if the organization spends only INR 50 lakh in that year.

### Reporting Requirements

#### FC-4 (Annual Return)

- **Who files:** Every person who has been granted FCRA registration or prior permission
- **When:** Within **9 months** of the closure of the financial year (i.e., by 31 December for FY ending 31 March)
- **Filed with:** MHA, via FCRA online portal (fcraonline.nic.in)
- **Contents:**
  - Details of foreign contribution received (donor-wise)
  - Details of utilization (activity-wise)
  - Assets created from foreign contribution
  - Administrative vs program expenditure breakdown
  - Bank account details and balances
  - Details of transfer to other FCRA-registered entities (if sub-granted)
- **Must file even if:** No foreign contribution was received during the year (a "nil" return)
- **Penalty for late/non-filing:** Suspension or cancellation of registration; late fee of INR 10,000 (if filed within 365 days of due date)

#### FC-6 (Intimation of Foreign Contribution)

- **Who files:** Any person receiving foreign contribution as a one-time grant or in specific circumstances (e.g., articles, securities)
- **When:** Within 30 days of receipt
- **Purpose:** Intimation to MHA about receipt of specific foreign contributions
- **Particularly relevant for:** Individuals, academics, and organizations receiving foreign hospitality or gifts

#### FC-3 (Application for Registration / Prior Permission)

- **FC-3A:** Application for FCRA registration
- **FC-3B:** Application for prior permission
- Both filed online via the FCRA portal

### Sub-Granting Rules

**Pre-2020 amendment:** Organizations could freely sub-grant (transfer) foreign contribution to other FCRA-registered organizations.

**Post-2020 amendment (Section 7 amended):**
- **Sub-granting is now prohibited.** An FCRA-registered entity cannot transfer foreign contribution to any other person/entity
- This was one of the most disruptive changes for the sector, as many large NGOs operated as "pass-through" entities, receiving funds from foreign donors and sub-granting to smaller implementing partners
- **Exception:** Transfer to organizations with prior permission, where the transferor is specifically named in the prior permission application (this is a narrow exception and subject to MHA interpretation)

**Practical impact:**
- Large international NGOs that previously channeled funds through Indian intermediaries now must fund implementing partners directly (each partner needs its own FCRA registration/prior permission)
- Hub-and-spoke implementation models have been severely disrupted
- Many organizations have restructured to either directly implement programs or help partners obtain their own FCRA
- Some organizations have shifted to domestic funding models for sub-granting

### Renewal Process

**Timeline:**
- Application for renewal must be filed **at least 6 months before** the date of expiry of the current registration
- Renewal is for 5 years
- Filed online via FC-3A on the FCRA portal

**Requirements for renewal:**
- Valid FCRA registration (not suspended or cancelled)
- All FC-4 returns filed up to date
- No pending adverse observations or show-cause proceedings
- Organization must have utilized at least a "reasonable" portion of foreign contribution received (no exact threshold, but MHA uses this as a qualitative criterion)
- Compliance with the designated FCRA account requirement
- Compliance with the 20% admin cap

**Common issues:**
- Many organizations have faced delayed renewals, with MHA taking 1-2 years or more
- During pending renewal, MHA has periodically issued extensions (but not always)
- If renewal is not granted and registration lapses, the organization cannot receive foreign contribution until the matter is resolved

### FCRA Violations and Penalties

| Violation | Consequence |
|-----------|-------------|
| Receiving FC without registration/permission | Seizure of funds; penalty up to 5 times the amount; imprisonment up to 5 years |
| Misutilization of foreign contribution | Suspension (up to 360 days) or cancellation of registration; forfeiture of funds to the Consolidated Fund of India |
| Exceeding 20% admin cap | Show-cause notice; suspension; cancellation |
| Not maintaining designated FCRA account | Suspension; cancellation |
| Late/non-filing of FC-4 | Late fee of INR 10,000; suspension; cancellation |
| Providing false information in FC-4 | Penalty up to INR 1 lakh; imprisonment up to 3 years |
| Sub-granting in violation of Section 7 | Suspension; cancellation; forfeiture of funds |
| Engaging in prohibited activities (political) | Cancellation; forfeiture; imprisonment up to 5 years |
| Not informing change of office address, bank, etc. | Show-cause notice; suspension |

**Suspension process:**
- MHA issues a show-cause notice
- Entity must respond within 30 days
- MHA may suspend FCRA registration for up to 180 days (extendable to 360 days)
- During suspension, entity cannot receive or utilize foreign contribution (except for ongoing committed expenditure with MHA permission)
- After investigation, MHA may either revoke suspension or cancel registration

**Cancellation:**
- Permanent revocation of FCRA registration
- All unutilized foreign contribution and assets acquired from FC are forfeited to the Consolidated Fund of India
- Organization must return any foreign contribution received after cancellation

### Prohibited Activities

FCRA foreign contribution **cannot** be used for:
- Activities that are political in nature (widely interpreted by MHA)
- Activities detrimental to the national interest
- Activities that affect the sovereignty and integrity of India
- Activities that have the potential to disturb communal harmony or public order
- Activities that incite an offence

**Persons prohibited from receiving FC:**
- Candidates for election
- Correspondents, columnists, editors, publishers of registered newspapers
- Judges and government servants
- Members of any legislature
- Political parties and their office bearers
- Organizations of a political nature (as determined by MHA)

### Interaction with Income Tax

- FCRA-registered organizations typically also hold **12A** (income tax exemption) and **80G** (donor tax deduction) registrations
- Foreign contribution income is exempt from tax if the organization has 12A registration and complies with Section 11/12 of the Income Tax Act
- Separate books of accounts must be maintained for foreign and domestic funds
- The Income Tax department and MHA share data; non-compliance with one can trigger scrutiny under the other

### Key Compliance Calendar

| Due Date | Compliance |
|----------|-----------|
| Within 30 days of receipt | FC-6 intimation (for specific receipts) |
| 31 December (each year) | FC-4 annual return for the FY ending 31 March |
| 6 months before expiry | Renewal application (FC-3A) |
| Within 15 days | Intimate change of designated person, bank account, address |
| Ongoing | Maintain designated FCRA account at SBI New Delhi |
| Ongoing | Maintain separate books of accounts for FCRA funds |
| Ongoing | Keep administrative expenditure within 20% |

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Assuming any bank account can receive foreign funds.** Post-2020, all foreign contributions MUST first land in the designated SBI New Delhi account. Receiving FC in any other account is a violation.

2. **Confusing FCRA with FEMA.** FEMA (Foreign Exchange Management Act) governs foreign exchange for trade and investment. FCRA governs foreign donations/grants for non-profit purposes. An NGO receiving a foreign grant needs FCRA compliance, not FEMA.

3. **Sub-granting without realizing it's prohibited.** The 2020 amendment banned transfers of foreign contribution between entities. Organizations that historically operated hub-and-spoke models must restructure.

4. **Miscalculating the 20% admin cap.** The cap is on foreign contribution *received* in the year, not on total expenditure or on the opening balance. If an org receives INR 50 lakh in FC but spends INR 1 crore (including prior year balance), admin expenses cannot exceed INR 10 lakh (20% of INR 50 lakh received).

5. **Not filing nil FC-4 returns.** Even if no foreign contribution was received in a year, the FC-4 must be filed. Non-filing can lead to cancellation.

6. **Mixing domestic and foreign funds.** FCRA mandates separate books of accounts and separate bank accounts for foreign and domestic funds. Commingling is a violation that can trigger suspension.

7. **Ignoring the renewal timeline.** Applications must be filed 6 months before expiry. Late applications risk a lapse in registration, during which the organization cannot legally receive foreign funds.

8. **Treating CSR funds as foreign contribution.** CSR funds from Indian companies (even Indian subsidiaries of foreign companies) are domestic contributions and not subject to FCRA. However, funds from a foreign company's headquarters (not its Indian subsidiary) would be foreign contribution.

9. **Not updating MHA on changes.** Changes to office address, bank accounts, key personnel, or objectives must be intimated to MHA within 15 days. Failure to do so is a compliance lapse.

10. **Political activity interpretation.** MHA interprets "political activity" broadly. Advocacy campaigns, lobbying for policy change, or involvement in politically sensitive issues can be construed as political activity, risking FCRA cancellation.
