## Domain Knowledge

### Grant Lifecycle

The grant lifecycle is the end-to-end process from identifying a funding opportunity to closing a grant. In the Indian development context, the typical stages are:

#### 1. Concept Note / Expression of Interest
- Short document (2-5 pages) outlining the problem, proposed intervention, target geography, estimated budget, and organizational credentials
- Used by donors to shortlist potential grantees before inviting full proposals
- Some donors issue a Request for Proposals (RFP) or Call for Applications instead

#### 2. Full Proposal
- Detailed document including:
  - Problem statement and needs assessment
  - Theory of Change / Logical Framework
  - Detailed activity plan with timeline
  - Results Framework with indicators, baselines, and targets
  - Detailed budget with justification
  - Organizational capacity statement
  - Sustainability plan
  - Risk assessment and mitigation
- Typically 15-50 pages depending on the donor's requirements

#### 3. Due Diligence
- Donor conducts organizational, financial, and programmatic due diligence
- See "Due Diligence" section below for details
- May include site visits, reference checks, and financial audits

#### 4. Approval and Grant Agreement
- Internal donor approval (CSR Committee for corporates; Board/program team for foundations)
- Grant Agreement / Letter of Agreement (LoA) / MoU is signed
- Defines deliverables, timelines, budget, reporting requirements, IP, termination clauses

#### 5. Disbursement
- Fund release as per the disbursement schedule in the grant agreement
- Common models: advance-based, reimbursement-based, milestone-based, or hybrid
- First tranche typically released upon signing; subsequent tranches linked to utilization and reporting

#### 6. Implementation
- Grantee executes activities as per the approved work plan
- Donor provides technical support, monitoring visits, and course corrections
- Mid-term reviews may trigger changes to scope, budget, or timeline

#### 7. Monitoring and Reporting
- Periodic reports (monthly/quarterly/annual) submitted to donor
- Financial reports: Statement of Expenditure (SoE), Fund Request (FR), Utilization Certificate (UC)
- Programmatic reports: progress against indicators, case studies, success stories
- Donor conducts independent monitoring visits

#### 8. Evaluation
- Mid-term evaluation (for multi-year grants)
- End-term/endline evaluation
- May be internal (by grantee), external (independent evaluator), or joint
- Assesses relevance, effectiveness, efficiency, impact, and sustainability (OECD-DAC criteria)

#### 9. Closure and Settlement
- Final report submission (programmatic and financial)
- Final Utilization Certificate
- Return of unspent funds (or agreement on carryover)
- Asset transfer/handover (if applicable)
- Grant closure letter from donor
- Audit completion

### Theory of Change (ToC)

A Theory of Change is a comprehensive description and illustration of how and why a desired change is expected to happen in a particular context. It maps the causal pathway from activities to long-term impact.

**Components:**
- **Problem statement:** The issue being addressed
- **Target population:** Who will benefit (direct and indirect beneficiaries)
- **Long-term goal (Impact):** The ultimate change sought
- **Outcomes:** Intermediate changes that contribute to the goal
- **Outputs:** Direct products of activities
- **Activities:** What the program does
- **Inputs:** Resources required (money, staff, materials)
- **Assumptions:** Conditions that must hold true for the causal chain to work
- **External factors:** Contextual conditions outside the program's control

**Structure (bottom-up):**
```
Inputs → Activities → Outputs → Outcomes → Impact
         (what we do)  (what we  (changes in  (long-term
                        produce)  behavior,    societal
                                  practice,    change)
                                  condition)
```

**Why it matters:** A well-articulated ToC helps stakeholders understand the logic of the intervention, identify measurement points, and recognize where assumptions might fail.

### Logical Framework (LogFrame)

The LogFrame is a matrix that operationalizes the Theory of Change into a planning, monitoring, and evaluation tool.

**Standard 4x4 LogFrame matrix:**

| Level | Narrative Summary | Objectively Verifiable Indicators (OVIs) | Means of Verification (MoV) | Assumptions |
|-------|-------------------|------------------------------------------|-----------------------------|-------------|
| **Goal (Impact)** | Long-term change the project contributes to | Impact-level indicators | National surveys, census data | External factors beyond project control |
| **Purpose (Outcome)** | Specific change the project will achieve | Outcome-level indicators | Evaluation reports, beneficiary surveys | Conditions for outcomes to lead to goal |
| **Outputs** | Tangible deliverables of project activities | Output indicators (quantitative) | Project MIS, attendance records | Conditions for outputs to lead to outcomes |
| **Activities** | What the project does | Activity completion metrics | Progress reports, financial records | Conditions for activities to produce outputs |

**Vertical logic:** Reading bottom-up, IF activities are completed AND assumptions hold, THEN outputs are produced. IF outputs are produced AND assumptions hold, THEN outcomes are achieved. And so on.

**Horizontal logic:** For each level, the narrative summary is measured by OVIs, which are verified through MoV, subject to assumptions.

### Results Framework

A Results Framework is the operational backbone of M&E. It specifies what will be measured, how, when, and by whom.

**Components:**
- **Indicators:** Specific, measurable variables that track progress
  - **Process indicators:** Track implementation (e.g., number of training sessions conducted)
  - **Output indicators:** Track deliverables (e.g., number of people trained)
  - **Outcome indicators:** Track changes (e.g., percentage increase in income)
  - **Impact indicators:** Track long-term effects (e.g., poverty rate reduction)
- **Baselines:** Starting values before the intervention
- **Targets:** Expected values at specific time points
- **Data sources:** Where the data comes from
- **Data collection methods:** How data is gathered
- **Frequency:** How often data is collected
- **Responsibility:** Who collects and reports the data
- **Disaggregation:** By gender, age, geography, social category, disability, etc.

**Indicator quality criteria (SMART):**
- **S**pecific — clearly defined, unambiguous
- **M**easurable — quantifiable or assessable
- **A**chievable — realistic given resources and context
- **R**elevant — directly linked to the objective
- **T**ime-bound — has a defined timeframe

### Monitoring and Evaluation (M&E)

**Monitoring** is the ongoing, systematic collection of data on project indicators to track progress and inform management decisions. It is continuous and real-time.

**Evaluation** is the periodic, in-depth assessment of a project's relevance, effectiveness, efficiency, impact, and sustainability. It is episodic and analytical.

**OECD-DAC Evaluation Criteria (revised 2019):**
1. **Relevance:** Is the intervention doing the right things?
2. **Coherence:** How well does the intervention fit with other interventions?
3. **Effectiveness:** Is the intervention achieving its objectives?
4. **Efficiency:** How well are resources being used?
5. **Impact:** What difference does the intervention make?
6. **Sustainability:** Will the benefits last?

**M&E Plan elements:**
- Results Framework with indicators, baselines, and targets
- Data collection plan (methods, tools, frequency, responsibility)
- Data quality assurance procedures
- Reporting templates and schedule
- Evaluation plan (timing, scope, methodology)
- Budget for M&E activities (typically 5-10% of total program budget)
- Learning and adaptation strategy

### Baseline, Midline, and Endline Studies

| Study | Timing | Purpose |
|-------|--------|---------|
| **Baseline** | Before intervention begins (or early in implementation) | Establish starting values for all indicators; understand pre-intervention conditions |
| **Midline** | Midway through the project (typically for 3+ year projects) | Assess early progress; inform course corrections; validate ToC assumptions |
| **Endline** | At or near project completion | Measure final values of indicators; compare with baseline to assess change; inform evaluation |

**Design considerations:**
- Use the same methodology across all three studies for comparability
- Include both quantitative (surveys) and qualitative (FGDs, case studies, KIIs) methods
- Ensure representative sampling
- Plan for panel data (same respondents) where possible for stronger attribution
- Budget for all three at the outset — retrofitting a baseline after the project starts severely weakens evidence

### Participatory Approaches

Development programs increasingly emphasize participatory methods:

- **Participatory Rural Appraisal (PRA):** Community-led assessment of needs, resources, and priorities
- **Participatory M&E:** Involving beneficiaries in defining indicators, collecting data, and analyzing results
- **Community Score Cards:** Beneficiaries rate service quality and accessibility
- **Social Audits:** Community-led review of program expenditure and outcomes
- **Most Significant Change (MSC):** Qualitative method where stakeholders identify the most significant changes resulting from the program
- **Outcome Harvesting:** Working backwards from observed changes to determine program contribution
- **Beneficiary Feedback Mechanisms:** Structured channels for beneficiaries to provide feedback, complaints, and suggestions

### Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) Mapping

The 17 SDGs provide a universal framework that many donors and programs use for alignment:

| SDG | Goal | Common Program Areas |
|-----|------|---------------------|
| 1 | No Poverty | Livelihoods, social protection, financial inclusion |
| 2 | Zero Hunger | Nutrition, food security, agriculture |
| 3 | Good Health and Well-being | Health services, WASH, maternal health |
| 4 | Quality Education | Schooling, skill development, digital literacy |
| 5 | Gender Equality | Women's empowerment, GBV prevention, SRHR |
| 6 | Clean Water and Sanitation | WASH infrastructure, hygiene behavior change |
| 7 | Affordable and Clean Energy | Clean energy access, energy efficiency |
| 8 | Decent Work and Economic Growth | Employment, enterprise development, skill building |
| 9 | Industry, Innovation, and Infrastructure | Digital infrastructure, technology access |
| 10 | Reduced Inequalities | Inclusion, disability, marginalized communities |
| 11 | Sustainable Cities and Communities | Urban planning, disaster preparedness |
| 12 | Responsible Consumption and Production | Sustainable livelihoods, waste management |
| 13 | Climate Action | Climate adaptation, resilience, carbon reduction |
| 14 | Life Below Water | Coastal livelihoods, marine conservation |
| 15 | Life on Land | Forest conservation, biodiversity, land restoration |
| 16 | Peace, Justice, and Strong Institutions | Governance, legal aid, anti-corruption |
| 17 | Partnerships for the Goals | Multi-stakeholder partnerships, data for development |

**SDG mapping in practice:**
- Most social programs contribute to 2-5 SDGs (primary and secondary alignment)
- Donors increasingly require SDG alignment in proposals
- India's NITI Aayog publishes state-level SDG indices that can inform program design
- CSR Schedule VII activities map to SDGs (e.g., Schedule VII item 1 maps to SDGs 1, 2, 3)

### Social Return on Investment (SROI)

SROI is a framework for measuring and accounting for the broader concept of value created by a program — including social, environmental, and economic outcomes.

**SROI process:**
1. **Establishing scope and stakeholders** — who is affected and how
2. **Mapping outcomes** — for each stakeholder, what changes
3. **Evidencing outcomes** — data on what actually happened
4. **Establishing impact** — adjusting for deadweight (what would have happened anyway), displacement, attribution, and drop-off
5. **Calculating SROI** — monetizing outcomes and comparing to investment
6. **Reporting and embedding** — communicating findings and using them for decision-making

**SROI ratio:** If a program invests INR 10 lakh and creates INR 35 lakh of social value, the SROI ratio is 3.5:1.

**Limitations:**
- Monetization of social outcomes is inherently subjective
- Requires significant data and expertise
- Not all outcomes can be meaningfully monetized
- Different methodologies yield different ratios, making comparisons unreliable

### Stakeholders in Development Programs

| Stakeholder | Role | Key Interests |
|-------------|------|---------------|
| **Donor** | Provides funding; sets strategic priorities | Impact, compliance, value for money, visibility |
| **Implementing Partner (IP)** | Executes programs on the ground | Funding continuity, organizational growth, mission alignment |
| **Sub-grantee** | Implements specific components under an IP | Capacity building, compliance, community access |
| **Beneficiary** | Direct recipient of program services | Quality services, dignity, voice, sustainable change |
| **Community** | Broader population in the target geography | Non-exclusion, positive externalities, no harm |
| **Government** | Policy maker, regulator, service provider | Convergence, data, compliance, scalability |
| **Technical Agency** | Provides specialized expertise (evaluation, training) | Quality, methodology rigor, publication |
| **Multilateral Organization** | UN agencies, World Bank, etc. — may fund or co-implement | Country strategy alignment, global standards |

### Types of Grants

| Type | Description | Typical Use |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **Project Grant** | Funding for a specific, time-bound project with defined deliverables | Most common; 1-3 year programs with clear scope |
| **Core/Institutional Grant** | Unrestricted funding for organizational operations | Rare; usually from long-term philanthropic partners |
| **Capacity Building Grant** | Funding to strengthen organizational capabilities | Systems, training, governance, financial management |
| **Seed Grant** | Small, initial funding to test an idea or pilot | Innovation, proof of concept; INR 5-25 lakh typical |
| **Scale-up Grant** | Larger funding to replicate a proven model | Expansion to new geographies or populations |
| **Emergency/Humanitarian Grant** | Rapid funding for disaster response | Shorter timelines, simplified reporting |
| **Research Grant** | Funding for studies, evaluations, or knowledge generation | Academic institutions, think tanks |

### Grant Instruments

| Instrument | Nature | Typical Use |
|------------|--------|-------------|
| **Letter of Agreement (LoA)** | Simpler, less formal; often for smaller grants | INR 5-50 lakh grants; short-duration projects |
| **Memorandum of Understanding (MoU)** | Statement of intent; less legally binding | Partnerships, collaborations, government partnerships |
| **Grant Agreement** | Formal, legally binding contract | Larger grants; multi-year programs; institutional donors |
| **Sub-grant Agreement** | Agreement between primary grantee and sub-grantee | Pass-through funding arrangements |

**Key clauses in grant agreements:**
- Scope of work and deliverables
- Budget and disbursement schedule
- Reporting requirements and templates
- Monitoring and evaluation provisions
- Intellectual property and data ownership
- Termination and dispute resolution
- Anti-corruption and anti-terrorism clauses
- Compliance with applicable laws (CSR, FCRA, etc.)
- Audit rights
- Asset management and disposition

### Due Diligence

Due diligence is the process of assessing an implementing partner's capacity, credibility, and risk profile before awarding a grant.

**Three dimensions:**

#### Organizational Due Diligence
- Legal registration and validity (Society/Trust/Section 8; active status)
- Governance structure (Board composition, meeting frequency, conflict of interest policies)
- Organizational history and track record
- Key personnel credentials and stability
- DARPAN/NGO-DARPAN registration
- IT Act registrations (12A, 80G)
- FCRA registration (if applicable)
- CSR-1 registration (if implementing CSR projects)

#### Financial Due Diligence
- Audited financial statements (last 3 years)
- Financial management systems and controls
- Bank account details and signing authorities
- Fund utilization track record
- Audit observations and management responses
- Tax compliance (TDS, GST if applicable)
- Asset register and inventory management
- Insurance coverage

#### Programmatic Due Diligence
- Relevant sector experience (thematic area, geography, target population)
- Past project outcomes and impact
- M&E capabilities and data management systems
- Community relationships and social capital
- Field presence and infrastructure
- References from other donors
- Conflict of interest assessment
- Safeguarding policies (child protection, POSH, anti-exploitation)

### Risk Assessment and Mitigation

| Risk Category | Examples | Mitigation Strategies |
|--------------|----------|----------------------|
| **Financial** | Fund misuse, delayed disbursements, exchange rate fluctuations | Milestone-based disbursement, regular audits, financial controls |
| **Operational** | Staff turnover, implementation delays, procurement issues | Succession planning, buffer timelines, procurement guidelines |
| **Programmatic** | Low beneficiary participation, unintended consequences, model failure | Community engagement, adaptive management, pilot testing |
| **Compliance** | FCRA violations, CSR non-compliance, tax issues | Compliance calendar, legal advisory, regular reviews |
| **Reputational** | Safeguarding incidents, media scrutiny, stakeholder conflicts | Safeguarding policies, grievance mechanisms, communication plans |
| **External** | Natural disasters, political instability, pandemic, policy changes | Contingency planning, flexible budgets, scenario planning |
| **Sustainability** | Benefits end when funding stops | Exit strategy, government convergence, community ownership |

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Conflating outputs with outcomes.** Training 500 people (output) does not mean 500 people improved their livelihoods (outcome). Agents must clearly distinguish between what a program produces and what changes as a result.

2. **Treating the Theory of Change as a one-time exercise.** A ToC should be a living document, revisited as evidence emerges and context changes. It is not just a proposal requirement.

3. **Over-relying on quantitative indicators.** Numbers tell you what happened but not why. A robust M&E plan balances quantitative indicators with qualitative methods (case studies, FGDs, MSC) to understand context and causality.

4. **Ignoring assumptions in the LogFrame.** The assumptions column is not decorative. If key assumptions fail (e.g., government policy changes, community resistance), the entire causal chain breaks.

5. **Treating all SDGs as equal in a program.** A livelihood program primarily contributes to SDG 1 (No Poverty) and SDG 8 (Decent Work). Claiming alignment with 10+ SDGs dilutes credibility.

6. **Confusing monitoring with evaluation.** Monitoring tracks whether activities are happening as planned. Evaluation assesses whether they are making a difference. Both are needed; neither substitutes for the other.

7. **Designing M&E after implementation starts.** Without a baseline, you cannot measure change. M&E planning (especially baseline surveys) must happen before or at the very start of implementation.

8. **Underestimating due diligence.** In India, many registered NGOs exist on paper but lack implementation capacity. Thorough due diligence — including field visits and reference checks — is essential.

9. **Assuming sustainability without a plan.** Every program should have an exit strategy from day one. Common approaches include government convergence (linking beneficiaries to government schemes), community ownership (forming and training local institutions), and market-based models.

10. **Using SROI without understanding its limitations.** SROI ratios vary wildly based on methodology and assumptions. A ratio of 5:1 does not mean a program is "better" than one with 3:1. Use SROI for internal learning, not competitive comparison.
